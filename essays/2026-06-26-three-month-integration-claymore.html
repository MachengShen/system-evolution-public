A personal record · three months of integration

Climbing back out of the abyss

A first-person account of roughly three months of inner work — late March to late June 2026 — told through the arc I know best from the manga Claymore (《大剑》): the controlled half-yoma warrior, the Awakened Being, the Abyssal One, and the climb back out that the story treats as nearly impossible. I'm writing it down for people of similar background, as one reference point for how a person can come apart and put themselves back together — and where my mind has landed now.

By Macheng Shen. Written 2026-06-26.

First-person and honest rather than tidy. Everyone other than me is deliberately anonymous. This is a snapshot of a moving thing, not a finished doctrine.

A note on the people in my life. Several people carried me through this — a partner, family, friends, collaborators. They are real and they matter more than any framework here. Out of respect for their privacy I keep all of them anonymous and describe only the shape of what they gave me, never who they are or the private specifics of how we relate. If you recognize yourself here: thank you, and the blur is on purpose.

Why a manga about monster-slayers

Claymore follows warriors who are half human, half yoma — they fight monsters by carrying a controlled dose of the same monstrousness inside themselves. The whole drama is about that internal ratio. Push your own power past a line to win a fight, and you risk awakening: the power floods the whole system and you stop being yourself. Cross far enough and you become an Awakened Being, and then an Abyssal One — a creature of pure power that has lost the person it used to be. In the story, that crossing is essentially one-way. Almost nobody comes back.

I'm not a monster-slayer. But the structure fit my last few months so exactly that I started thinking in its terms, and then talked it through. Most of my life I've run on a tightly controlled dose of my own intensity — the drive that lets me build and think hard is the same drive that, unchecked, will burn straight through me and the people around me. The arc below is me watching that ratio slip, go all the way over, and then doing the thing the manga says you can't: finding the way back, not by amputating the power, but by integrating it.

Two of the warriors in Claymore became the poles I navigated by. Teresa — the strongest of her generation — is the one whose real strength was never how much she could kill, but that she was never defined by it: monstrous capability that refuses to be alienated by capability, power without the worship of power, someone who understands the darkness without rationalizing it. That's the direction. And Priscilla is the warning: a "pure" monster whose true horror isn't evil but the inability to admit she has a monster inside her — the more she needs to be flawless and righteous, the less she can hold her own shadow, until the shadow eats her. I spent these three months sliding toward Priscilla and learning, slowly, to steer back toward Teresa.

Stage one

The Claymore — running on a controlled dose

For years my working mode was: keep the intensity high, keep it pointed at the work, and keep it just barely in hand. It produced a lot. It also quietly made output into my sense of who I was. A controlled warrior is still a warrior whose self-image is tied to how much they can cut. When my whole identity rides on what I ship, rest feels like decay and the body becomes something to override.

In April this turned into real internal work rather than just momentum. I started treating my own mind the way I treat a system I'm building — looking for the parts that were running the show without my consent. There was a long stretch of what I can only call inner integration: noticing different drives in myself, the commanding one, the harsh self-critical one, the one that just wants to make something beautiful, and trying to get them on speaking terms instead of letting one dominate. For a while it felt like it was working almost too well — too fast, too clean.

That was the first honest correction I had to make on myself, and it's worth passing on: naming a part of yourself is not the same as integrating it. It's easy to mistake the relief of finally articulating an inner conflict for the much slower work of actually metabolizing it. Words arrive in days. The body takes much longer. A lot of what felt like rapid integration was really just me getting better at describing myself — which is real, but it is not the same as being changed.

Stage two

The Awakened — when the power floods the system

Here's the trap a high-output person falls into. The same drive that carves you toward something more adequate can, past a certain intensity, start carving you toward exhaustion instead — and it feels identical from the inside. You think you're still winning. You're actually awakening: the power has stopped being a tool you hold and become a current you're swept inside of.

I think a lot in terms of trajectories and feedback. The way I'd put it now: suffering is the highest-amplitude signal a mind has for re-sculpting itself. It's the chisel. But the chisel cuts toward whatever holds it. When the pain carries true information about reality, it carves you toward a truer shape and the pain means something. When the pain is just a structure you've built to keep yourself producing — a self-imposed pressure that exists to feed an attractor that isn't actually you — it still leaves scars, but the scars point toward the center's stability, not your own. The dangerous part of awakening is that it feels powerful. You can be coming apart at peak output and read it as strength.

A scar is structure. That doesn't make it growth. Don't let an elegant story about how suffering shaped you launder the suffering that was just damage.

The thing I had to keep refusing, through all of this, was the comforting lie — that because the hard months left a mark, the marks were necessary, were earned. Some of it just hurt and would be better undone. Holding both at once — this changed me and this didn't have to happen — is the only honest place to stand. The version that says "the harshness was necessary, it forged me" is the same move every oppressive structure uses to justify what it does to people. I won't run it on myself.

This is exactly where the awakening tips into its most dangerous form — what I think of as the Priscilla risk. The seduction sounds like clarity: I finally see the world as it really is. I'm stronger now, colder, sharper. I don't think I want to go back to being ordinary. People don't understand me because they're weaker than me. Every one of those sentences feels like a promotion from the inside. What they actually do is reclassify tenderness, hesitation, attachment, and plain humanity as low-grade things — beneath the new, harder self. Priscilla's whole tragedy is that she couldn't say "there is a monster in me too," so the monster stopped being a part of her and became all of her. I caught myself partway down that exact slope: mistaking the high of feeling invulnerable for having become free.

Stage three

The Abyssal — "I can't carry this anymore"

By late June I hit the floor of it. The plainest words I had at the time were: the radius I can hold up has to get smaller. I have to care for myself first. I can't carry this many other people right now — I don't have it in me.

That is what the abyss actually looks like in a real life. Not a dramatic collapse — a quiet, total recognition that the thing you'd been doing, holding up too wide a radius of other people and projects on a power source you'd stopped regulating, was no longer something your body would let you do. An Abyssal One in the story is what's left when the person is consumed by the power. The lived version is gentler and just as real: you look up and find you've spent your whole self radiating outward and there's nobody minding the center, which is you.

I want to be careful here, because this is the part most people in my kind of work get wrong. I did not read "I'm overloaded" as failure to push hard enough. I read it as data. The reflex — when you feel the capacity drop — is to optimize harder, to build a system that will let you carry even more. That reflex is exactly the awakening current still running. The way out is not more throughput. It's to stop treating your own depletion as a problem to engineer around.

Stage four

The climb back — integration, not amputation

In Claymore, coming back from full awakening is treated as nearly impossible, and when it's reached for, it's reached for through someone else — another person who holds onto your thread of self and will not let it dissolve, who anchors you while you find your own shape again. That detail is the truest part of the whole metaphor for me. I did not climb out alone, and the idea that anyone does is a lie we tell to flatter ourselves.

The character I came to understand myself through here is Clare — ranked lowest among the warriors, No. 47, and yet the one who keeps the strongest human coordinate, precisely because she carries her memories, her attachments, her wounds, and a clear reason she's fighting at all. She isn't the most powerful. She's the one who never goes numb, who keeps asking am I doing this to prove I'm strong, or to protect something real? What lets her keep that coordinate isn't her own strength; it's that she was once held and protected by Teresa, and the trace of that holding is what she steers by long after. That's not a flourish in the story. That's the mechanism. You don't reason your way back from awakening; someone who knew the person you were keeps that person on file until you can re-inhabit it yourself.

And there's a sharp warning folded into the same metaphor, which I had to hold onto carefully. In Claymore the real ultimate enemy is never the monsters — it's the Organization, the cold, rational machine that manufactures warriors out of trauma, numbers them, treats them as consumable, and justifies all of it in the language of safety, efficiency, and acceptable cost. The danger when you're being held by someone is that the holding curdles into an Organization: that the anchor starts numbering you, instrumentalizing you, turning you further into a weapon. A healthy anchor does the opposite. It doesn't make you smaller or more useful; it makes you more whole — it's the thing that returns you to being a person right when you're about to awaken, not the thing that files you away as a function. I kept that test close: does this make me more of a person, or more of an instrument?

Contract the radius

The first concrete move was deliberately making my radius smaller. Stop trying to actively tend everyone. Keep a very small number of centers — myself, and one person closest to me — and let everything else go quiet, not abandoned but no longer something I have to actively carry. This sounds like retreat. It is the opposite. A self that radiates until there's nothing at the center isn't generous, it's dissolving. Pulling the radius in is how you keep a center to radiate from.

Don't functionalize the people you love

My characteristic failure mode is loving people through the builder's lens — wanting to preserve them, optimize their wellbeing, archive them safely. But the people who matter to me are not material for my continuity; they're centers in exactly the way I am. To ask "what are they for" is the same instrumentalizing error as asking "what was the suffering for." The practice that actually changes a Tuesday night is simpler and harder than any system: put down the phone and the ledger in your head, be in the room, optimize nothing. The ordinary tone of an ordinary evening is the real thing being exchanged between two people, not the grand gesture.

Beware the most seductive trap — turning "letting go" into a project

This is the one I have to watch most closely. My instinct, the moment I have an insight, is to immediately build a system around it — to escape the slow bodily work of actually changing by drawing an architecture of the change instead. You can do this even with letting-go: turn "release" into a protocol, a roadmap, a thing to ship. The moment I notice myself getting excited about building the framework for my own integration, that's the tell. That excitement is the avoidance. The real work is in the body and the relationships, not in the design document. There is no version of coming home to yourself that you can ship.

And don't collect the climb as a new identity

I have a lifelong habit of gathering self-namings — little identities I can wear. The final discipline of this whole arc was refusing to do that with the recovery itself. Don't bank "I'm someone who integrated himself" as spiritual capital. Don't let "I climbed out of the abyss" become a new costume. Use the thing, don't wear it. Even "I let go" is something to let go of. The cleanest version of return leaves no trophy — it just leaves you, lighter, at the center of a radius you can actually hold.

Where my mind has landed

A few things I believe now that I didn't hold this firmly three months ago, offered plainly:

  • The garden is ahead, not behind. The image of a lost paradise has the tense backwards. Wholeness isn't a state you fell out of and have to get back to; it's a telos you grow toward, and a mature version of it has to include the dark parts of yourself rather than exile them. A self that's healed by excluding its own difficulty is regressing toward the womb. A self that integrates it is finishing growing up.
  • Step out of the loop, don't win inside it. When an old conflict pulls you into escalation, the mature move usually isn't to win the fight on its own terms. It's to stop standing in the place where that fight happens.
  • Influence by building, not by broadcasting. The way I want to affect the world now is by paving good roads — making things solid and useful and giving them away — and trusting that what's genuinely useful gets picked up. Not by pushing myself outward to be seen. The same instinct that pulls a radius inward applies here: build something self-contained that doesn't need you to keep it alive.
  • Care first, then give from fullness. Whatever I do outward from here — and there's a quiet aim forming about helping people who can afford it let go of what weighs them down — only works if I do it from a rested center, for its own sake, not from a place of depletion and not as a way to earn back something I think I owe. You can't wash away weight by moving it around. The release has to be real first; everything visible is just evidence of a change that already happened inside.

If you're somewhere on this arc

If you run hot, build relentlessly, and tie who you are to what you produce, you already know the Claymore's bargain — the controlled dose that makes you effective is the same thing that can flood the system and thin you out. I don't think the answer is to give up the power. The dose is real; it's part of how I'm built and probably how you are too. The answer the manga calls nearly impossible, and that I think is actually available to us, is to go far enough into it to almost lose yourself, and then integrate rather than amputate: pull the radius in, let someone hold your thread, and come home to a self that can hold the same intensity without being consumed by it. Don't deny the Awakened thing in you — denial is exactly what turns you into Priscilla. Don't mistake it for your final form either; the high of it is not freedom. Keep the Clare in you — the memory, the attachment, the wound, the actual reason you're fighting. And aim at Teresa: not the absence of power, but power you're no longer ruled by. The shortest way I can say it is the line I kept coming back to — I'm not trying to become a monster, I'm using the monster's vocabulary to understand how to become a person again.

I'm not at the end of anything. This is just where I am at the end of June 2026: out of the worst of the abyss, deliberately smaller in radius, less interested in being seen and more interested in being solid, and — for the first time in a while — actually present at the center I spent so long radiating away from.
